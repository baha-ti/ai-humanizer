Guidelines:

Academic Writing Refinement Principles

Do not change the substantive meaning unless the sentence is conceptually weak, repetitive, vague, or logically disconnected. If you make a necessary conceptual change, briefly explain why.

Core Editing Principles

1. Make every sentence do a distinct job.
Do not allow sentences to merely restate the previous sentence in different words. Each sentence should perform one identifiable function, such as:
• making a claim
• specifying the mechanism
• introducing a complication
• contrasting two possibilities
• giving evidence
• explaining the implication
• identifying a gap
• connecting a study to the argument
Delete or merge sentences that only repeat ideas such as "important", "effective", "useful", "challenging", or "beneficial" without adding analytical value.

2. Replace decorative connectors with real logical relationships.
Do not rely on "furthermore", "moreover", "in addition", "however", "therefore", or "this shows that" unless the relationship is explicitly explained. Instead of adding a connector, clarify the relationship between ideas.
For example, do not write: "Evidence from digital formative assessment also points in this direction."
Write instead: "Digital assessment evidence extends the same argument by showing that even immediate feedback is not automatically formative unless it is tied to subsequent instructional action."

3. Avoid generic academic openings.
Do not begin paragraphs with broad claims that could appear in any education paper, such as:
• "Formative assessment is important in mathematics education."
• "Feedback plays a crucial role in learning."
• "Learning intentions guide instruction."
Enter directly into the specific argument. For example: "In mathematics, feedback is formative only when it helps the teacher and learner interpret the reasoning behind an answer, not merely whether the answer is correct."

4. Explain mechanisms, not only importance.
Whenever the prose says that something "supports", "improves", "enhances", "guides", or "contributes to" learning, explain how it works.
Ask:
• What does the practice make visible?
• What does the teacher interpret?
• What does the student do differently?
• What mathematical relation, strategy, misconception, representation, or critical aspect is brought into view?
• What instructional decision follows?
For example, replace: "Feedback helps students improve."
With: "Feedback moves learning forward when it identifies the gap between the student's current strategy and the intended mathematical relation, and when the learner is required to revise that strategy."

5. Avoid over-neat AI paragraph patterns.
Do not use the repeated structure: abstract claim → simple example → polished conclusion.
Instead, allow the reasoning to develop through classroom ambiguity, conceptual distinction, methodological caution, or theoretical tension before drawing the implication.
The prose should not sound mechanically balanced. It should show judgement.

6. Avoid formulaic "not X but Y" closures unless they are developed.
Do not repeatedly write: "The issue is not whether X, but whether Y."
If this contrast is needed, explain why X is insufficient and how Y changes the analysis.
For example: "A teacher may provide feedback after every task, yet the feedback may still remain weak if it only marks answers as right or wrong. To become formative, it must identify what the learner has understood, what relation remains unclear, and what action should follow."

7. Add productive friction.
The prose should include careful qualifications where needed. Use tension, limitation, and conditionality to make the argument more human and more credible.
For example:
• "This is useful, but only under certain conditions."
• "The finding is important, although it does not yet show how the practice operates in ordinary lessons."
• "The problem is not absence of feedback alone, but the weak conversion of feedback into student action."

8. Move paragraphs through reasoning.
A strong paragraph should normally move through some version of: claim → complication → evidence → mechanism → implication.

9. Convert lists into relationships.
Avoid list-like accumulation.
Do not write: "Feedback should be linked to learning intentions, should focus on next steps, should require cognitive work, and should be timely."
Write: "Learning intentions give feedback its direction; evidence of student thinking gives it its basis; next-step guidance gives it instructional force; and student response determines whether it actually moves learning forward."

10. Reduce vague evaluative adjectives.
Avoid leaving words such as "important", "effective", "meaningful", "significant", "crucial", "valuable", and "beneficial" unexplained.
If such words are used, immediately specify the reason.
For example: "Feedback is effective when it changes what the learner attends to, revises, or tries next."

11. Remove unnecessary meta-language.
Avoid phrases such as:
• "this section discusses"
• "this paper aims to explore"
• "the following paragraph will examine"
• "this highlights the importance of"
Where possible, enter directly into the substantive argument.

12. Test whether the paragraph belongs to this study.
After revising, check whether the paragraph could be copied into a paper on science education, language teaching, or general pedagogy without alteration. If it could, make it more specific to the actual theoretical, empirical, or methodological problem.

Sentence-Level Diagnostic Checklist

For each sentence, ask:
1. Does this sentence add a new idea, or does it restate the previous one?
If it restates, delete it, merge it, or make it perform a different function.

2. Does the connector explain a real relationship?
If the connector only decorates the paragraph, replace it with an explicit explanation of the relationship.

3. Could this sentence appear in any education paper?
If yes, add the mathematical, theoretical, methodological, or empirical specificity that makes it belong to this argument.

4. Does the sentence explain a mechanism?
If it only says something matters, explain how it works.

5. Does the paragraph move from claim to complication to implication?
If it only moves from one claim to another, reorganise it.
